Abstract

Bitcoin enables peer-to-peer payments without relying on a central intermediary. Proof-of-work secures the ledger so anyone can verify supply and settlement.

Core mechanics

  • Blocks & proof-of-work: Hashing links blocks together and makes history expensive to rewrite.
  • Distributed nodes: Anyone can verify transactions and enforce consensus rules independently.
  • Difficulty adjustment: Keeps block production steady around ten minutes despite shifting miner power.

Why it matters

  • Predictable supply capped at 21 million BTC.
  • Open participation without gatekeepers.
  • Auditability that rivals traditional financial ledgers.

Chapter-by-chapter in simple terms

Shareable notes you can use to brief stakeholders or onboard teammates quickly.

Transactions

Ownership transfers through digital signatures. Transactions chain together so the network can verify who controls a given output.

Timestamp server

Each block timestamps a batch of transactions. The chain of timestamps proves the order of events.

Incentives

Miners earn block rewards and fees for securing the chain. Incentives align honest behavior with economic gain.

Privacy

Pseudonymous addresses and best practices like avoiding address reuse keep observers from linking identities to balances.

Network

Nodes broadcast transactions, validate them, and accept the longest valid chain as the source of truth.

Conclusion

Bitcoin combines cryptography, networking, and incentives to create digital cash that runs without central control.
